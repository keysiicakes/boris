Correlation of Confidence and Incompetence: Incidences of the Dunning-Kruger Effect Among the Students of Claro M. Recto Senior High School

Chapter 1 Introduction

Studies have shown that people lacking intellect or mastery tend to overestimate their abilities, leading to flawed self-assessment and false assumptions that they are better than the average person, due in part to their lack of metacognition or self-awareness needed to perceive their own ignorance (Dunning, 2011). Self-monitoring is essential in effectively controlling one’s own behavior and attitudes in learning. In this paper, we will review the empirical and real-world evidence of the Dunning-Kruger Effect and the better-that-average phenomenon, and its applicability in the context of Filipino senior high school students of Claro M. Recto Senior High School.

Background of the Study

Accurate self-assessment can lead to academic improvement (Papanthymou & Darra 2018) and overall better learning outcome. However, people, especially those who are novices in their field, lack the necessary facilities to recognize their ignorance necessary for the accurate estimation of their own abilities. This is due in part to their lack of metacognition or self-awareness needed to perceive their own ignorance (Dunning, 2011).

Incidences of the Dunning-Kruger effect in the real world is observed by numerous studies across different fields: a substantial number of individuals believe that their financial situation is better than average (Barrafrem et. al., 2020), people tend to underestimate their bias and racial prejudice, believing themselves to be more tolerant than average (West, 2019), and English language students in higher education who grossly underperformed in demonstrating information literacy has no calibration of their own skills, overestimating their abilities in self-assessment (Mahmood, 2016).

This also applies in the field of healthcare and medicine: a study of 639 healthcare workers showed that 74 percent reported self-compliance to hand hygiene protocols, but only 51 percent positively reported their colleagues (Kelcikova et. al, 2019). Moreover, people who lack the knowledge of basic facts and dismissal of misinformation tend to think that they are better-informed than healthcare professionals (Motta et. al., 2018). These incidences of failing to see one’s ignorance in linked to the nature of ignorance itself: according to assertions made by David Dunning, people suffer from the double burden of being incompetent and being ignorant of their own ignorance. “Instead, they often believe they act with adequate if not excellent expertise, when instead they misstep out of misunderstanding and miscalculation that they fail to recognize as such.” (Dunning, 2011) This unknown unknowns make it hard for people to recognize incompetence when they lack the facilities in the subject to begin with.

The Dunning-Kruger Effect (DKE) is demonstrated in people's ability to recognize the perceived accuracy and integrity of the media (Stimmers, 2020), which affects the people's ability to hone information literacy, which, in turn, allows them to be targets of fake news (Jones-Jang et al. 2021).

Theoretical Framework

This study will be guided by the empirical studies published by D. Dunning in the journal “Advances in Experimental Social Psychology” (2011) where he asserts two things about ignorance: its prevalence in our daily lives, and its invisibility from those who suffer it. Dunning states: “...any individual’s mental catalogue of their areas of ignorance is likely to be very incomplete… because many of their deficits are camouflaged in one of two ways. First, many instances of ignorance fall into the category of unknown unknowns. Second, many instances of ignorance may be obscured because they are hidden behind misbeliefs that people mistake for valid knowledge in the domain in question.” (Dunning, 2011)
